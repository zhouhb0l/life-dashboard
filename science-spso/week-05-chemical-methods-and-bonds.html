Atoms, Molecules, And Ions

Chemistry explains matter by looking at particles. The scan begins by separating three basic particle ideas: atoms, molecules, and ions.

Atoms

Atoms are the smallest particles of an element. Noble gases are monatomic elements, meaning they exist naturally as single atoms.

Molecules

A molecule is a group of two or more atoms that are chemically combined. Molecules may be elements or compounds.

Ions

An ion is a charged particle that can make up compounds, such as sodium chloride, NaCl.

Diatomic And Polyatomic Element Molecules

Diatomic examples from the scan

Hydrogen, iodine, chlorine, and oxygen commonly appear as two-atom molecules: H2, I2, Cl2, and O2.

Polyatomic examples from the scan

Sulfur, phosphorus, ozone, and fullerene can form molecules with more than two atoms, such as O3 and C60.

Important distinction: a molecule can be an element, like O2, or a compound, like water, H2O. A compound always contains at least two different elements chemically combined.

Compounds And Chemical Formulae

A compound is a pure substance containing two or more elements chemically combined in a fixed ratio. Its chemical formula tells us both the types of atoms and the ratio of the different atoms present.

Water

Water is H2O: two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom in each molecule. Electrolysis can break water down into hydrogen and oxygen gases.

Sodium chloride

Common salt has properties very different from sodium metal and chlorine gas because the atoms become ions in an ionic lattice.

Added explanation: "Broken down" for a compound means its chemical bonds are changed. A physical method such as filtering cannot turn water into hydrogen and oxygen, because the atoms are chemically bonded.

Mixtures

A mixture is made up of two or more substances that are not chemically combined. The components are not fixed and can be present in any ratio.

Heterogeneous mixtures

The composition is non-uniform. Examples from the scan include cereal in milk, ice in soda, soil, and blood.

Homogeneous mixtures

The composition is uniform. Examples from the scan include vodka, air, rainwater, and steel.

Alloy question from the scan: an alloy is a mixture, not a compound. Many classroom alloy examples are homogeneous solid mixtures, although real alloys can sometimes contain more than one phase.

Methods Of Separating Mixtures

Mixtures can be separated because their substances keep their own physical properties. The right method depends on differences in magnetism, solubility, particle size, boiling point, movement through paper, or density.

Magnet

Separates magnetic solids, such as iron filings, from non-magnetic solids.

Filter / sediment

Separates an insoluble solid from a liquid by trapping the solid or allowing it to settle.

Evaporation

Removes solvent to recover a dissolved solid, especially when the solid is stable on heating.

Centrifuge

Spins mixtures so denser particles move outward faster and separate.

Chromatography

Separates soluble substances that move at different speeds through paper or another stationary phase.

Distillation

Separates liquids or recovers solvent using differences in boiling point.

Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons and electrons, but a different number of neutrons. Because neutron number changes, isotope mass changes too.

Same

Number of protons, number of electrons, chemical identity, and most chemical behaviour.

Different

Number of neutrons and mass. Radioactivity may differ between isotopes.

Stable isotope

A stable isotope is not radioactive. The scan notes that there are about 400 stable isotopes.

Examples from the scanClassificationNote
Chlorine-35 and chlorine-37Stable isotopesSame 17 protons; different neutron numbers.

Iodine-131, uranium-235, uranium-238Radioactive isotopesUseful in medicine, nuclear science, or dating, but require radiation safety.

Allotropes

Allotropes are different structural forms of the same element. Because atoms are connected or arranged differently, allotropes can show very different physical properties and chemical behaviours.

Carbon

Graphene, nanotube, fullerene, diamond, and graphite.

Oxygen

Oxygen gas O2 and ozone O3.

Sulfur

Different sulfur structures can form under different conditions.

Phosphorus

White, red, and black phosphorus. White phosphorus glows greenish in oxygen and is pyrophoric.

Electron Configuration

The arrangement of electrons around the nucleus determines many chemical properties of an atom. Each shell holds a maximum number of electrons and has a different energy level. In this introductory model, the first shell holds 2 electrons, the second holds 8, and the third is treated as holding 8 for the first 20 elements.

Valence shell

The outer shell, furthest from the nucleus.

Valence electrons

Electrons in the outer shell. They control many reactions and bonding patterns.

Period

Horizontal rows of elements in the periodic table are called periods.

Group

Vertical columns are groups. Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons in this model.

Stable Configurations: Group 0 Noble Gases

Noble gases are unreactive because their outer shells are full. Helium has a full first shell of 2 electrons; neon and argon have full outer shells of 8 electrons.

Chemical Bonds: Ionic Bonding

Atoms without full outer shells are reactive. To achieve a stable noble-gas-like electronic configuration, atoms may lose, gain, or share electrons. When atoms lose or gain electrons, they form ions.

Cation

A positive ion. Metals often form cations by losing valence electrons, such as Na+.

Anion

A negative ion. Non-metals often form anions by gaining electrons, such as Cl-.

Ionic bond

The strong electrostatic force of attraction between positive and negative ions.

Source sentence, sharpened: when a metal reacts with a non-metal, metal atoms transfer valence electrons to non-metal atoms. Ions are formed, and both sides can reach a stable electronic configuration.

Covalent Bonding: Sharing Electrons

A covalent bond is formed by sharing electrons between two atoms. This often happens between non-metal atoms. The scan uses hydrogen, chlorine, oxygen, and water as examples.

Small correction to remember: the scan lists oxygen, O2, under "common compounds", but oxygen gas is not a compound. It is a molecule of an element because it contains only oxygen atoms.

Metallic Bond, Metals, And Alloys

A metallic bond is the force of attraction between positive metal ions and the "sea" of mobile electrons. This model explains why metals often conduct heat and electricity, and why they can be malleable and ductile.

Metal Properties From The Scan

  • Good conductors of heat and electricity.
  • High density.
  • High melting and boiling points.
  • Malleable and ductile.
  • Pure metals may react with air and water, and may wear away easily.

Alloys

An alloy is a mixture of a metal with one or a few other elements. Alloys can make metals harder and stronger, lower melting points, improve appearance, and make metals more resistant to corrosion.

Corrosion And Rusting

Rusting, or the corrosion of iron, is the process that produces rust. The scan lists the conditions needed for rusting: iron, oxygen, and water.

How To Prevent Rusting

  • Keep iron dry or away from oxygen.
  • Paint, oil, grease, or plastic-coat the surface.
  • Galvanise with zinc or use sacrificial protection.
  • Use stainless steel or another corrosion-resistant alloy.

Rust formula idea: rust is hydrated iron(III) oxide, often written approximately as Fe2O3.xH2O. Salt water speeds rusting because ions help charge move through the water.
